History

  1. Six Approaches to Psychology:

    • Psychoanalytic: Focuses on the unconscious mind, dreams, and inner conflicts as determinants of behavior and mental processes.
    • Behavioral: Examines observable behaviors and how they are influenced by stimuli and responses in the environment.
    • Gestalt: Studies how people perceive and interpret the world as a whole, emphasizing patterns and context.
    • Humanistic: Emphasizes personal growth, self-actualization, and the inherent goodness of individuals.
    • Cognitive: Investigates mental processes such as thinking, memory, and problem-solving.
    • Biological: Explores the biological basis of behavior, including genetics, brain structure, and neurotransmitters.

  2. Scientific Approach:

    Psychology uses a scientific approach to study behavior and mental processes. Key differences:

    • True Experiment: Manipulates variables to establish causation.
    • Correlational Approach: Examines relationships between variables without manipulation.

  3. Key Figures in Psychology:

    • William James: Pioneer of functionalism, focusing on how mental processes function in adapting to the environment.
    • Wilhelm Wundt: Founder of structuralism, the study of the basic elements of consciousness.
    • Charles Darwin: Influential in psychology for his ideas on natural selection and individual differences.
    • Sir Francis Galton: Contributed to the study of individual differences. Cousin of Darwin.
    • John Watson: Known for behaviorism, emphasizing observable behaviors.
    • Titchener: Prominent in structuralism, studying the structure of consciousness.

  4. Introspection:

    A method where individuals reflect and report on their own thoughts, feelings, and sensations.

  5. Testable Hypothesis:

    A hypothesis in science should be disconfirmable or falsifiable, meaning it can be tested to either support or refute it. Avoid offering explanations after observing results.

  6. Random Assignment:

    In true experiments, subjects are randomly assigned to groups to control for extraneous variables, ensuring that differences in the dependent variable are due to the independent variable.

  7. Random Sampling:

    In research, a random selection of subjects helps create a sample that represents the larger population, allowing for the generalization of findings.

  8. True Experiments vs. Correlational Studies:

    True experiments involve manipulation to establish causation, while correlational studies examine relationships without manipulation.

  9. Fields of Specialization:

    Study various fields within psychology as outlined in the textbook.

  10. Charles Darwin and Psychology:

    Darwin's theory of natural selection highlighted the role of individual differences, which later led to the study of individual differences in psychology.

  11. Ethics of Research:

    Considerations include the Institutional Review Board (IRB), informed consent, minimizing harm, privacy, and debriefing of participants.

Statistics and Methods

  1. Area Under the Normal Curve:

    The phrase "Area Under the Normal Curve" means understanding the percentages associated with different standard deviations from the mean on a normal distribution curve, which are approximately 34% within one standard deviation, 14% within two standard deviations, 2% within three standard deviations, and 0.13% beyond three standard deviations from the mean. This knowledge is essential for interpreting data and making statistical inferences in the context of a normal distribution.

  2. Dependent and Independent Variables:

    The dependent variable is what you measure, and the independent variable is what you manipulate in an experiment.

  3. Descriptive vs. Inferential Statistics:

    Descriptive statistics summarize data, while inferential statistics draw conclusions or make predictions based on data.

  4. Measures of Central Tendency and Spread:

    Central tendency includes mean, median, and mode. Spread involves range, standard deviation, and variance.

  5. Manipulating a Variable:

    To manipulate a variable means intentionally changing it to observe its effect on the dependent variable.

  6. Standard Deviation:

    A measure of the spread or variability of data points in a dataset. It indicates how much individual data points differ from the mean.

  7. Correlation:

    It has magnitude (strength) and direction (positive or negative). A correlation of 0 means no relationship, while -0.95 is a strong negative correlation.

  8. Z-Score:

    Also called standardized score, it allows the comparison of scores from different scales or units by converting them into a common scale.

    The formula for calculating a z-score is: z = (x-μ)/σ
    Where: x is the raw score, μ is the population mean, and σ is the population standard deviation. For example, if you have a test score of 190, and the test has a mean of 150 and a standard deviation of 25, your z score would be: z = (190 – 150) / 25

  9. Third Variable Problem and Directionality Problem:

    These are issues in correlational studies that can make it tricky to figure out what's really causing what.

    • Third Variable Problem: Imagine you see a link between two things, like ice cream sales and drownings. But the real reason might be a third thing, like hot weather, which makes people buy ice cream and swim more. So, it's not the ice cream causing drownings.
    • Directionality Problem: Sometimes, you know two things are related, but you're not sure which one comes first. Like, do happy people make more friends, or do having more friends make people happier? It's like a chicken-and-egg situation; it's hard to tell which came first.

    These problems remind us that correlation doesn't always mean causation.

  10. Research Methods:

    • Surveys: Surveys are like questionnaires. We ask people questions to collect information about their thoughts, feelings, or behaviors. It's like asking a bunch of people what they think.
    • Observational Method: This is when we watch and take notes. We observe people or things to understand how they act or behave. It's like watching a soccer game and writing down what players do.
    • Case Studies: Case studies are like detailed stories about one person or a small group. We study them closely to learn about specific situations or people. It's like reading a detailed biography about someone's life.
    • Correlational Method: This is about finding relationships between things. We see if two things are connected, like if studying more leads to better grades. It's like noticing that when it's sunny, more people go to the beach.
    • Experimental Method: Experiments are like science tests. We change something on purpose to see how it affects something else. It's like testing if watering plants more makes them grow faster.
    • True Experiment: In true experiments, we carefully control everything to see if one thing causes another. We use groups of people and change one thing to check if it makes a difference. It's like testing if a new medicine really cures a sickness by giving it to one group and not the other.

Brain and Behavior

  • Nervous System:

    Know the overall organization, including the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) and peripheral nervous system (sensory and motor neurons).

  • Neuron:

    Understand its components, including dendrites (receive signals), soma (cell body), axon (transmits signals), terminal buttons (release neurotransmitters), and myelin sheath (insulation).

  • Neurotransmitters and Synapse:

    Neurotransmitters transmit signals across synapses, the gaps between neurons.

  • Cerebellum and Corpus Callosum:

    The cerebellum coordinates motor skills, and the corpus callosum connects the brain's hemispheres.

  • Lobes of the Cerebral Cortex:

    • Frontal Lobe (Motor): Responsible for planning, decision-making, and voluntary muscle movements.
    • Temporal Lobe (Auditory): Involved in processing auditory information (hearing) and some aspects of memory.
    • Parietal Lobe (Sensory): Processes sensory information like touch, temperature, and spatial awareness.
    • Occipital Lobe (Visual): Primarily responsible for processing visual information and recognizing shapes, colors, and objects.


    • Broca's and Wernicke's Areas: These brain regions are critical for language production (Broca's) and comprehension (Wernicke's).

    Central Fissure and Mapping:

    • Motor Cortex: Command center; sends signals for muscle movement.
    • Sensory Cortex: Data receiver; senses information from body's sense (touch, sight, sound, etc).

  • Brain Plasticity:

    The brain's ability to reorganize and adapt, especially after injury.

  • Left and Right Hemispheres:

    • Left Hemisphere: Handles analysis, verbal activities (like talking, understanding, speech, reading, writing).
    • Right Hemisphere: Manages synthesis, putting elements together, perceiving the whole, creating maps, and forming 3D sketches.

  • Roger Sperry's Split-Brain Research:

    Studied patients with split brains to treat epilepsy, revealing the specialization of each hemisphere.

  • Visual Agnosia and Prosopagnosia:

    Visual agnosia is the inability to recognize objects, while prosopagnosia is the inability to recognize faces.

Perception

    Illusory Contours: Perceiving contours where none exist.

    Figure/Ground Discrimination: Distinguishing between the main object (figure) and its background (ground).

    Gestalt Grouping Laws: Principles that explain how we organize visual information into meaningful wholes.

    • Law of Similarity: Similar things belong together.
    • Law of Proximity: Things close together look related.
    • Law of Closure: We tend to mentally complete incomplete shapes.
    • Law of Continuity: Our eyes prefer smooth, continuous lines or curves.
    • Law of Common Fate: Objects moving together seem related.

    Bionocular Cues Visual information that enable depth perception.

    • Binocular Disparity: Each eye sees a slightly different view, and your brain uses the differences to gauge depth.
    • Convergence: Your eyes move closer together when you look at something close, and your brain uses this closeness to understand depth.

    Monocular Cues: Visual cues that help us see depth using only one eye.

    • Motion Parallax: Things closer to you seem to move faster than distant objects when you move your head.
    • Elevation Above Horizon: Objects higher on your field of vision appear farther away.
    • Interposition: When one object partially covers another, you perceive the covered object as closer.
    • Linear Perspective: Parallel lines appear to converge or meet in the distance.
    • Aerial Perspective: Distant objects look fuzzier and bluer due to the air between you and them.
    • Relative Brightness: Brighter objects appear closer, and dimmer ones seem farther away.
    • Texture Gradient: Details on surfaces become less distinct with distance.
    • Shading: The way light and shadows fall on objects helps your brain perceive their three-dimensional shape and depth.

  • Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up Processing:

    Top-down involves using prior knowledge, while bottom-up starts with raw sensory input.

  • Ames Room Illusion:

    An optical illusion where objects appear distorted due to the room's design.

  • Perceptual Constancies:

    Understanding that objects maintain their characteristics (size, color, shape) even when viewed from different angles or lighting conditions.
